It's been seven years [since I last blogged]({{< ref "posts/cottonwood-art-fest-2019.md" >}}) about the [Cottonwood Art Festival](https://cottonwoodartfestival.com/). We've been back several times since then, sometimes with the kids (now much older) and sometimes just Kassi and I, and sometimes, like this year, just me and one of the older kids. But I never bothered writing any more about it, not being much of a consistent blogger and all. And to be honest, some years at the festival have been better than others. The event is put on twice a year in spring and autumn, with several artists being regular attendees, so you can tend to see a lot of the same stuff, but for the most part they keep up a very lively and healthy roster. You have to qualify before a panel of judges to be allowed to exhibit, so the standard of quality is higher than average. Kassi tells me we've never actually been to the fall show, though I don't know how in all these years we've never managed it. 

This year I'm happy to say we really enjoyed ourselves. And for the first time in ages I took my little pocket camera for some old-school blogging action.

Part of the reason we had such a good time was because Augie and I started off with a visit to the food trucks, of which there was a respectable variety. American standard fair food, Texas elote trucks, barbecue, fried Louisiana style treats, black soul food, you name it. We each ordered fajita nachos while we listened to the original pop stylings of upcoming(?) singer songwriter [Lexi Lee Gamble](https://www.instagram.com/lexileegamble/). Not quite my speed music-wise, but if she ever makes it big we can say we were listening to her back when she was working the art festival circuit. After hanging around and digging that for a bit we made our way straight over to the craft beer and margarita station, were I had a draft dunkel of some variety and Augie opted for a frozen margarita. Then we were off to browse some art.

And to be honest I almost strictly mean _browse_, as we are by no means what you would call art collectors. We do occasionally pick something up that catches our eye, if the price is reasonable, but that kind of casual art appreciator is not the crowd this festival is meant to appeal to. This stuff is generally high dollar. We're talking large statement pieces, meant for big walls and galleries. And we do have enough of a market in Dallas for all that to make the show pretty lucrative for some of these folks, but mostly we are there for the entertainment and inspiration. For the imaginative possibilities. For instance, I met a photographer one year with a nice collection of photos, some of which were in theme and quality very similar to [my own](https://glass.photo/tobyblog). So I had a conversation with him about his life as a photographer, how he got into the business, and the places that it had taken him, and it turns out he'd retired from his IT job in Dallas just a few years back to pursue his dream. That kind of haunted me, but it also lit a little fire within me. Would it really be possible? I keep telling myself one of these days I'll take my first steps.

Who knows? Maybe this year will be the year.

And that's another thing I find really cool about this festival — many of the artists are very happy to converse with you about their work and lives. You meet many interesting and talented folks. Some of whom are quite well known and exhibited widely, like [Marlene Rose](https://marlenerose.com/).

As we were winding down our visit we got caught up in conversation with this one guy, [Chris Robleski](https://www.theflashnites.com/), who assisted by his wife Katie specializes in a unique kind of light painting photography. I'm not going to lie, I at first just assumed it was all done in Photoshop, but my scoffing was very quickly rebuked by copious signage pointing to the fact that he does all the lightwork by hand, using very simple lighting tools (mostly colored flashlights), and everything is done in-camera, like a real artist.

When I say you meet some interesting characters at the festival he would be a standout example, as the guy has lived all over the US, played professional hockey in his youth, has kicked and presumably _can still kick_, a lot of ass, was trained and worked as an architect in SoCal, and is generally a very chill dude and almost a Gen-X archetype, whether he realizes it or not. Augie especially was very into his work which coincidentally was among the most reasonably affordable pieces at the festival. I asked him about, and he was willing to share a great deal about, his life and work as a professional photographer, which I frankly found fascinating. He publishes a newsletter at [his website](https://www.theflashnites.com/), which I highly recommend you check out.

And if that wasn't a great enough way to end things, on the way out we stumbled on the booth of of [Brandon O'Neill](https://www.instagram.com/puzzle_palette/), a local artist who specializes in what I'd describe as 2D pixel art sculpture made entirely of out puzzle cubes. He had a few cubes on display of various dimension, so I told him that my son used to solve these things as a hobby when he was younger. Augie asked if he could have a go at the 5x5, and the guy told him encouraged him to. He had never seen a rando solve one in the wild before, so it was a real treat for him. He had to step in at one point, Augie having forgotten the old solving algorithms from years back, but together they were able to make the solve. I think we made each other's day. 

Enjoy the photos. We're going to try for another visit in the fall.
