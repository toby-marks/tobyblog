It's been seven years [since I last blogged]({{< ref "posts/cottonwood-art-fest-2019.md" >}}) about the [Cottonwood Art Festival](https://cottonwoodartfestival.com/). We've been back several times since then, sometimes with the kids (now much older) and sometimes just Kassi and I, and sometimes, like this year, just me and one of the older kids. But I never bothered writing any more about it, not being much of a consistent blogger. And to be honest, some years at the festival have been better than others. There are a few of these artists that return year after year, so you can tend to see a lot of the same stuff, especially considering that the show is hosted twice a year in spring and fall. Kassi tells me we've never actually been to the fall show, though I don't know how in all these years we've never managed it. 

That being said, we had a good time.

Maybe it was because Augie and I started off with a visit to the food trucks, of which there was a respectable selection. American standard fair food, Texas elote trucks, barbecue, fried Louisiana style treats, black soul food, you name it. We each ordered fajita nachos while we listened to the original pop stylings of upcoming(?) singer songwriter [Lexi Lee Gamble](https://www.instagram.com/lexileegamble/). If she ever makes it to the big time we can say we were listening to her back when she was playing the Cottonwood Art Festival. After digging that for a bit we made our way straight over to the craft beer and margarita station, were I had a draft dunkel of some variety. Augie opted for a frozen margarita and then we were off to browse some art.

And to be honest I almost strictly mean _browse_, as we are by no means art collectors. We do occasionally pick up something that catches our eye, if the price is reasonable, but that kind of casual appreciator is not the crowd this festival is meant to appeal to. The visual art is typically high dollar, large statement pieces meant for big walls and galleries. And we do have enough of a market in Dallas for that kind of thing to make the show feasible, but mostly we are there for the entertainment and inspiration. For the imaginative possibilities. For instance, I met a photographer there one year with a collection of photos some of which were thematically similar to my own. So I had a conversation with him about his life as a photographer, how he got into the business, and the places that it had taken him, and it turns out he'd retired from his IT job in Dallas just a few years back to pursue his dream. That kind of haunted me, but it also lit a little fire within me. Would it really be possible? I keep telling myself one of these days I'll take my first steps.

Maybe this year will be the year.
